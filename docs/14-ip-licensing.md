# 14 — IP & Licensing: Classic Movie Monsters

Status: Reviewed v1.0 · **This document is general research, not legal advice.** Its conclusions are the brief for the professional clearance review budgeted below and tracked as Q6 in [12-open-questions.md](12-open-questions.md).

## Bottom line

**The game does not need to license any classic monsters — and should not try.** The design already wants archetypes, not replicas (pillar 1, [01-vision.md](01-vision.md)): the Mutator exists so no player fields a stock character. The legal posture is *pastiche with guardrails*, plus a modest professional clearance review before content lock.

## The key distinction: the character vs. the film design

Every classic movie monster has two IP layers:

1. **The underlying character/concept** — usually 19th-century literature or folklore, long in the **public domain**: Mary Shelley's *Frankenstein* (1818), Wells's *Invisible Man* (1897), Stoker's *Dracula* (1897), mummies and werewolves (folklore — unowned), giant-brain creatures (a generic 1950s B-movie trope — unowned).
2. **The specific film realization** — Universal's 1930s–50s designs, which Universal owns and actively enforces: Jack Pierce's flat-headed, neck-bolted Frankenstein makeup; the specific Gill-man suit; the "Universal Monsters" merchandising trademark. Actor likenesses (Karloff, Lugosi, Chaney) are separately protected by **rights of publicity**, enforced by estates, varying state by state.

We may freely ship a stitched-together reanimated corpse monster. We may not ship a recognizable replica of Karloff's flat head and neck bolts (yet — see timeline), and never under Universal-style branding.

## The public-domain conveyor belt

US copyright on the classic films runs 95 years from release:

| Film | US public domain |
| --- | --- |
| *Dracula*, *Frankenstein* (1931) | **Jan 1, 2027** |
| *The Mummy* (1932) | Jan 1, 2028 |
| *The Invisible Man* (1933) | Jan 1, 2029 |
| *Bride of Frankenstein* (1935) | Jan 1, 2031 |
| *The Wolf Man* (1941) | Jan 1, 2037 |
| *Creature from the Black Lagoon* (1954) | ~Jan 1, 2050 |

Given the roadmap ([11-roadmap.md](11-roadmap.md)), the iconic 1931 Frankenstein film design plausibly enters the US public domain before launch. Attorney-verification caveats: trademarks never expire (Universal can still police monster-branding and merchandising uses); later films' additions (e.g., the Bride's hair, 2031) stay protected on their own clocks; publicity rights are independent of copyright; non-US territories have different terms.

**The Gill-man is the standing exception**: a Universal original with no literary public-domain root, protected until ~2050. Amphibian-archetype monsters are fine; Gill-man replicas are not, period. Likewise the "giant brain" trope is free, but specific designs (e.g., the Metaluna Mutant, *This Island Earth*, 1955 — also Universal) are not.

## Why licensing from Universal is the wrong path

Major-studio character licensing for games typically means a six-to-seven-figure minimum guarantee, 10–20% royalties, approval rights over every asset and marketing beat, months of negotiation — and studios rarely license to unproven teams at all. The cost is not legal fees; it is minimum guarantees and an unreturned phone call. The design gains nothing from it: famous fixed characters would *undermine* pillar 1.

## What we can use freely

- The name **"Frankenstein"**, the doctor, the nephew-inherits-the-notebooks fiction — all Shelley, public domain everywhere ([01-vision.md](01-vision.md) frame is clear).
- Genre vocabulary nobody owns: fog, gothic labs, lightning, stitches, full moons, hunchbacked assistants, torch-bearing mobs.
- Archetypes: stitched reanimated bipeds, wolfish quadrupeds, bandaged shamblers, transparent horrors, amphibious lurkers, exposed-brain mutants — *as our own designs*.

## Art-direction guardrails (normative for the part catalog)

These rules bind part authoring in [08-creature-visualization.md](08-creature-visualization.md):

1. **Evoke the archetype, never the specific famous design.** If a part is recognizable as *the* movie version, redesign it.
2. **No flat-head-plus-neck-bolts combination** until the 2027 clearance is attorney-confirmed; no actor-likeness faces ever.
3. **No Gill-man replicas, period** (protected to ~2050). Amphibian archetype: yes; that suit: no.
4. **No "Universal Monsters"-adjacent branding, trade dress, or marketing framing.**
5. Every part-family concept sheet gets a one-line prior-art note at authoring time — this is what makes the eventual clearance review cheap.
